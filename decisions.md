## 2026-05-08 — Refund reversal walks billings oldest-first (FIFO unwind)
**What:** `allocateFifo` in [src/lib/ledger/fifo-allocation.ts](src/lib/ledger/fifo-allocation.ts) now reverses paid billings oldest-first when a `client_refund` exceeds available unallocated credit. Previously the loop ran backward (`for (let i = billings.length - 1; ...)`), reopening the newest paid billing first. Pure refactor of the existing loop; behaviour is observable only when a refund spans more than one billing slot. New section "11" in [fifo-allocation.test.ts](src/lib/ledger/fifo-allocation.test.ts) locks in the oldest-first unwind: two billings of 600 and 400, both fully paid by a single 1000 payment, then a 300 refund — b1 reopens to 300 outstanding, b2 stays paid in full.
**Why:** Forward consumption is FIFO (the allocator's name and existing test 9). Without an explicit policy on the reverse direction, the loop ran LIFO, producing an asymmetric contract: payments fund the oldest billing first, but refunds reopen the newest one. With both directions FIFO, the per-billing outstanding becomes a stable function of `(billings_received, payments_received - refunds_received)` regardless of when a refund landed in the timeline. Test 8 (single-billing refund, only path the prior tests covered) is unaffected since the loop direction is irrelevant when there's one slot.
**Alternatives:** (a) LIFO unwind — defensible because the most recent payment is the most likely target of a refund, but the `payment_allocations` table doesn't carry refund-source semantics so neither direction can claim "the refund was for this specific payment." Rejected to keep the allocator's forward and reverse direction uniform under one mental model. (b) Per-refund target field on the transaction (operator picks which billing the refund reverses) — out of scope; would require a new column and a UI affordance for a workflow that isn't yet a friction point. (c) Proportional unwind across all paid billings — rejected; produces fractional cents on every billing slot and obscures the "this billing went back to outstanding" signal in the per-shipment views.


## 2026-05-08 — Partner reimbursement allocator gains EPS tolerance
**What:** `allocatePartnerReimbursements` in [src/lib/ledger/partner-reimbursement-allocation.ts](src/lib/ledger/partner-reimbursement-allocation.ts) now uses `const EPS = 0.001` everywhere it previously compared `outstanding`/`remaining` against `0`. The final pass that flips `is_fully_settled` also zeros the `outstanding` field when it falls under EPS so the operator never sees `$0.00` paired with a "still owing" badge. New section "8" in [partner-reimbursement-allocation.test.ts](src/lib/ledger/partner-reimbursement-allocation.test.ts) regresses the case: a 100 USD claim covered by three payouts of 33.33 + 33.33 + 33.34 must mark the claim fully settled with zero unallocated payout.
**Why:** [installment-allocation.ts](src/lib/ledger/installment-allocation.ts) (real-estate, same allocator shape) already used EPS = 0.001; its header comment line 6 advertises that it "Mirrors the contract of `partner-reimbursement-allocation.ts`" — but the EPS treatment was added to one and not back-ported to the other. Without EPS, three payouts whose decimals don't divide evenly into the claim leave a sub-cent residue (typically ~1e-15 from float arithmetic) that keeps `is_fully_settled = false`, so the claim re-surfaces in the partners pending list with a `$0.00 outstanding` row the operator can't mark off. The fix aligns the two allocators on the same numerical contract.
**Alternatives:** (a) Round amounts to 2 decimals at insert time so the residue never accrues — rejected; the rest of the codebase preserves full precision for FX-conversion accuracy and trimming digits at the AP boundary would be inconsistent. (b) Tighten EPS to `0.0001` (sub-tenth-of-a-cent) — rejected; matches `installment-allocation.ts:41` at `0.001` instead, since both allocators feed the same UI conventions (`formatCurrency` rounds to 2 decimals anyway). (c) Skip the explicit `outstanding = 0` zeroing and let the renderer round — rejected; downstream code (e.g. `useOverdueInstallments` filter at `inst.outstanding <= 0.001`) reads the raw number and a stale residue would still flip the wrong predicate.

## 2026-05-08 — Contacts switch from single `type` to multi-role flags ("Business Partner")
**What:** Replaced the single `contacts.type` text column with five boolean columns (`is_customer`, `is_supplier`, `is_logistics`, `is_real_estate`, `is_other`) on the same row, each independently settable. A new check constraint `contacts_at_least_one_role` requires every row to have at least one role flag true. Backfill from the old `type` was 1:1 (no row played more than one role today). The legacy `type` column is left in place for one revision; a follow-up migration will drop it. Surface-level changes: the contact form's Identity step shows five toggleable role pills instead of a single Type dropdown ([src/features/contacts/contact-form-dialog.tsx](src/features/contacts/contact-form-dialog.tsx) — new inline `RolePicker`); contact list/cards/detail render one badge per role via a new `ContactRoleBadges` ([src/features/contacts/role-badges.tsx](src/features/contacts/role-badges.tsx) — replaces `type-badge.tsx`); contact detail stacks BOTH `ContactLedgerSection` and `SupplierLedgerSection` when both `is_customer` AND (`is_supplier` || `is_logistics`) are true. Nine cross-feature pickers/filters (products, orders, transactions ×3, contacts list, contact balances, transactions index color logic) swapped `.eq("type", "X")` → `.eq("is_X", true)`.
**Why:** Real-world contacts in trading/export commonly play more than one role (a company you both buy from and sell to). The old single-type model forced duplicate records, which fragmented address, tax_id, and ledger across two contact IDs and broke per-counterparty reporting. This is the same pivot SAP S/4HANA, Odoo, NetSuite, and Dynamics 365 made years ago — they call the unified record a "Business Partner" with role flags. We're standardising on that pattern. Migration was safe to do greedily because no contact today carries more than one role, so the backfill produces identical observable behavior; the new flexibility unlocks dual-role contacts going forward without retrofitting orders/payments/shipments (each already references contact by ID, not role).
**Alternatives:** (a) Junction table `contact_roles(contact_id, role)` — rejected; pure normalization, but every picker and filter would need a join or subquery, and 5 stable roles don't justify the indirection. Boolean columns on the parent row keep all queries single-table. (b) Two roles only (Customer/Supplier) plus a separate Category field for Logistics/Real Estate/Other — closer to the SAP "BP role + industry" split, and arguably more correct (a freight forwarder *is* a supplier whose category is logistics). Rejected because the user wants to keep the existing five buckets as peer flags rather than reclassify Logistics and Real Estate as supplier subtypes; matches their current mental model. (c) Drop the old `type` column in the same migration — deferred to a follow-up so we keep a one-step rollback if the multi-flag deployment surfaces a problem in production.


## 2026-05-08 — Currency-defining fields are locked once child rows exist
**What:** Added defensive guards to five mutation paths that previously let users edit a row's currency-defining field while child rows referenced it: (1) `updateAccount` ([src/features/accounts/mutations.ts](src/features/accounts/mutations.ts)) blocks `asset_code` change once any `treasury_movements` row points at the account; (2) `updateShipment` ([src/features/shipments/mutations.ts](src/features/shipments/mutations.ts)) blocks `invoice_currency` change once `status !== 'draft'` (i.e., the booked `shipment_billing` accrual exists); (3) `updateDeal` ([src/features/real-estate/mutations.ts](src/features/real-estate/mutations.ts)) blocks `currency` change once any transaction is linked to the deal via `real_estate_deal_id`; (4) `updateLoan` and `updatePsdEvent` leg edits ([src/features/partners/mutations/loans.ts](src/features/partners/mutations/loans.ts), [src/features/partners/mutations/psd-events.ts](src/features/partners/mutations/psd-events.ts)) now call the existing `assertAccountCurrencyMatches` (re-exported from `transactions/mutations.ts`) before swapping the linked treasury_movement's `account_id` to a new account; (5) `updateTransaction` ([src/features/transactions/mutations.ts](src/features/transactions/mutations.ts)) blocks `currency` / `contact_id` / `kind` change on a `supplier_invoice` once any `supplier_payment` row references it via `related_payable_id`. Each guard reads the existing row, compares to the incoming value, runs a count query against the child table only when the field is actually changing, and throws a user-facing error naming the affected count and the recovery path (deactivate the parent, detach payments first, etc.).
**Why:** `SUM(quantity)` in `computeBalanceMap` is currency-blind — it adds up the numeric `quantity` column without looking at the account's `asset_code`. So flipping an account from USD to EUR on a row with movements turns "1000 USD" into "1000 EUR" with no error, no warning, and no way to tell from a fresh ledger view that it ever happened. The same shape repeats across the codebase: shipment billing computes `sales` as a raw number and writes it under `shipment.invoice_currency`, real-estate FIFO sums receipt amounts currency-blind, `computeOutstandingByInvoice` does `invoice.amount - sum(payments.amount)` ignoring currency. Each spot needs the same guard at the *write* path because the *read* paths can't tell the corruption apart from legitimate data. The guards live in mutations rather than in DB triggers because (a) the messages need to name the affected children and a user-actionable recovery, which a CHECK constraint can't, and (b) the mutations are the only writers — direct service-role SQL is rare and the audit log catches it (2026-05-05 entry). The supplier_invoice variant mirrors the existing payment-side `validateRelatedPayable` check so the rule is symmetric: a payment can't link to a mismatched invoice, and an invoice can't drift away from its existing payments.
**Alternatives:** (a) Add CHECK constraints or triggers at the DB level that block the same edits — rejected for the message-quality reason above and because the constraint would need to know which other table to count in (`treasury_movements` for accounts, `transactions` for deals, `transactions` filtered by `related_payable_id` for invoices), which is the kind of join logic that's cleaner in app code. (b) Disable the field in the UI form when the parent has children — rejected as the only defense; UI lock-out helps but doesn't protect against URL-prefill, future bulk-update flows, or scripts. The mutation-side guard is the chokepoint that catches everything. (c) Re-denominate the children automatically on parent change (e.g., convert all linked transactions from EUR to USD using today's spot) — rejected; FX-converting *historical* movements at *current* rates is exactly the silent corruption we're trying to prevent. (d) Allow the change but force-detach children first as a side effect — rejected; deletion-as-a-side-effect of an edit is hostile UX, the user should opt in to detaching with full visibility into what they're losing. (e) Fold the five guards into one shared helper — deferred; the child-count query, the parent-table name, and the error message all differ per case, and the bodies are short enough that abstracting now would be premature.


## 2026-05-08 — `allocateFifo` emits `payment_allocations` for retroactively-matched prepayments
**What:** Restructured `allocateFifo` ([src/lib/ledger/fifo-allocation.ts](src/lib/ledger/fifo-allocation.ts)) so prepayments — payments that arrive before any billing — are tracked as a queue of `{event, remaining}` pairs (`pendingCredits`) instead of a single `unallocatedCredit` number. When a later billing creates a slot with `paid < billed`, the retroactive-match loop drains the oldest credits into that slot AND emits `payment_allocations` entries linking the prepayment event to the billing it funded. Refunds also drain from this queue (oldest credits first) before walking back through paid billings. The exported `unallocated_credit` total is now derived as `sum(pendingCredits[].remaining)`. The change is behind no flag — it just moves the allocator's output strictly closer to its docstring contract.
**Why:** Three downstream consumers depend on `payment_allocations` to attribute a payment to the shipment(s) it covers: the per-shipment "Payments applied" table ([src/features/shipments/payments-applied-table.tsx](src/features/shipments/payments-applied-table.tsx)), the contact ledger's per-row breakdown ([src/features/contacts/contact-ledger-section.tsx](src/features/contacts/contact-ledger-section.tsx)), and the customer-facing PDF statement ([src/lib/pdf/generate-shipment-statement-pdf.tsx](src/lib/pdf/generate-shipment-statement-pdf.tsx)). Pre-fix, `shipment_allocations[].paid_amount` correctly reflected retroactive draining (the totals were right) but `payment_allocations` stayed empty for the prepayment branch, so the per-shipment view rendered "No payments have been applied to this shipment yet" while the totals row read "fully paid" — a contradiction the user could only resolve by reading the contact ledger top-to-bottom. The customer's PDF was the worst case: a shipment paid 100% by a prepayment would be listed with `total_received = 0` and `balance = total_billed`, i.e., the customer would see an invoice claiming they hadn't paid. The fix preserves the FIFO contract (oldest credit consumed first, refunds reversing in the same order) and adds a regression test for the prepayment path. Test 9 in [src/lib/ledger/fifo-allocation.test.ts](src/lib/ledger/fifo-allocation.test.ts) now also asserts `payment_allocations.length === 2` and that p1 → b1 (6300) and p2 → b1 (14700) are emitted in queue order.
**Alternatives:** (a) Keep `unallocatedCredit` as a number and synthesize `payment_allocations` after the fact by re-walking the events — rejected; you'd need to re-derive which payment was the source of each cent of credit, and the queue is the cleanest representation of that fact already. (b) Emit the allocations only on the first allocation pass (skip retroactive matches) — rejected; that's exactly the bug. (c) Switch to a per-cent ownership model — rejected as overkill; the per-event queue captures everything we need at much lower complexity.


## 2026-05-08 — Installment status: overdue beats partial when both apply
**What:** Reordered the status-assignment branches in `allocateRealEstateInstallments` ([src/lib/ledger/installment-allocation.ts](src/lib/ledger/installment-allocation.ts)) and `computeLoanState` ([src/features/partners/queries/loans.ts](src/features/partners/queries/loans.ts)). The new precedence is: `paid > overdue > partial > due/open`. Pre-fix, both allocators evaluated `partial` before `overdue`, so an installment with `paid > 0`, `outstanding > 0`, AND `due_date < today` rendered as `partial` (amber) rather than `overdue` (red). Two existing tests in [src/lib/ledger/installment-allocation.test.ts](src/lib/ledger/installment-allocation.test.ts) were updated to assert the corrected status, and a new section "14b" was added to lock in the partially-paid-and-past-due case explicitly.
**Why:** A real-estate or loan installment that's been part-paid but is also late is the *most* delinquent state — the customer started paying, then stopped. Showing it as amber "partial" hides the actionable signal (chase the remainder) behind the green-tinted "they're making progress" framing. The deal-card consumer ([src/features/real-estate/deal-card.tsx](src/features/real-estate/deal-card.tsx)) had already worked around this by re-checking `(due_date < today && outstanding > 0)` directly, but the per-installment row ([src/features/real-estate/installment-row.tsx](src/features/real-estate/installment-row.tsx)) and the loan drawer used `state.status` straight, so the badge color disagreed with the deal-level rollup whenever this case occurred. Fixing the allocator at the source makes every consumer correct simultaneously. The deal-card workaround is left in place as belt-and-braces — it still produces the right answer and removing it would be a separate, riskier cleanup.
**Alternatives:** (a) Fix the consumers individually (badge color, deal status) instead of the allocator — rejected; that pushes the precedence rule into every renderer and guarantees future drift. The allocator is the one place that knows all four signals (paid, outstanding, due_date, today). (b) Add a new `partial_overdue` status — rejected; four buckets is the right granularity for this UI surface (paid / past-due / making-progress / not-yet-due), and a fifth would force every consumer's color map to grow. (c) Show both badges — rejected; the per-row layout is already cramped at the editorial-broadsheet column widths.


## 2026-05-07 — `ticker_registry` replaces hardcoded `COINPAPRIKA_IDS` for the crypto picker
**What:** Added a new `ticker_registry` table (`provider`, `code`, `slug`, `name`, `rank`, `last_seen_at`; PK `provider, code`) seeded daily from CoinPaprika's `/v1/coins` endpoint by the existing `refresh-rates` Edge Function (no new pg_cron schedule — the seeder is folded into the same weekday-06:00-UTC run). The account form's "Asset code" input becomes a searchable Combobox listing every active CoinPaprika ticker (~10,000 rows, sorted by rank popularity-first) when `asset_type='crypto'`. The price refresh path now resolves an account's `asset_code` to a CoinPaprika slug via the registry first, falling back to the original hardcoded `COINPAPRIKA_IDS` map only when the registry is empty (cold start) or the row hasn't been seeded yet. Also includes `20260507130001_ticker_registry_disable_rls.sql` to match dev-mode policy on the rest of the treasury tables.
**Why:** Adding a new crypto holding (e.g. PAXG, ARB, SUI) used to require a code change to extend the 20-entry `COINPAPRIKA_IDS` map, and the form silently accepted any free-text ticker — including typos that produced "No price source for: …" toasts forever. Yusuf shouldn't have to file a request to add a coin; if CoinPaprika prices it, the form should let him pick it. Storing the catalogue in Postgres (rather than as a JSON file shipped in the bundle) keeps the JS bundle size unchanged and lets the daily Edge Function refresh keep the list current. The `slug` column is what avoids a hardcoded map: when refreshPriceSnapshots iterates the user's crypto accounts, each `asset_code` looks up its slug in the registry and the price fetch proceeds without any developer intervention.
**Alternatives:** (a) Ship the CoinPaprika list as a static JSON in `src/features/treasury/coinpaprika-coins.json` — rejected; would inflate the JS bundle by ~300 KB on every page load and would freeze the catalogue at build time, requiring redeploys to add new tickers. (b) Hit CoinPaprika `/coins` on every form open — rejected; ~300 KB over the wire each time the dialog opens, no resilience to upstream outages, and CoinPaprika's free tier has rate limits. (c) Keep the hardcoded map and just make the form's helper text list the supported tickers — rejected; preserves the file-a-PR-to-add-a-coin friction the user explicitly asked to remove. (d) Build a TEFAS fund picker in the same pass — deferred; TEFAS lacks a clean equivalent of `/coins` and the user's fund universe is just 4 codes today, so the registry shape is in place (`provider='tefas'` is allowed by the CHECK) but seeded lazily in a follow-up.


## 2026-05-07 — PAXG priced as crypto (CoinPaprika), not as a metal
**What:** Added `PAXG: "paxg-pax-gold"` to `COINPAPRIKA_IDS` in [src/features/treasury/constants.ts](src/features/treasury/constants.ts). PAXG (Paxos Gold) is a tokenised gold holding — 1 PAXG = 1 troy ounce of allocated London gold — but for pricing purposes it is treated as a crypto, not a metal: it goes through CoinPaprika alongside BTC, ETH, USDC, etc., rather than through the gold-api.com / `METAL_ASSET_MAP` path used for `XAU` and `Altın`.
**Why:** PAXG is an ERC-20 token whose market price tracks gold but is not identical to spot — it carries an exchange premium/discount, on-chain liquidity effects, and an issuer custody fee. Using gold-api.com's XAU spot would systematically misvalue the holding. CoinPaprika gives the actual traded PAXG/USD which is what the user pays/receives when liquidating. It also keeps the data model honest: the holding lives in a Binance-style exchange account with `asset_type='crypto'`, and physical gold (`Altın`, `XAU`) stays in metal-typed accounts at physical custody locations — co-mingling them under `metal` would have made the treasury totals double-counted or confused.
**Alternatives:** (a) `asset_type='metal'`, `asset_code='XAU'` priced from gold-api.com — rejected, see above (mis-pricing + data-model conflation with physical gold). (b) Custom mini-API or hardcoded "1 PAXG ≈ 1 XAU" passthrough — rejected; CoinPaprika already returns the live PAXG quote and reusing the existing crypto path is a one-line change. (c) Leave PAXG out of `COINPAPRIKA_IDS` and accept the "No price source for: PAXG" toast — rejected; the holding is real and the user wants it in treasury USD totals.


## 2026-05-05 — Currency CHECK constraints; convention: shipment.invoice_currency is canonical
**What:** Added migration `20260505130000_currency_check_constraints.sql` introducing CHECK constraints (with `NOT VALID` so existing rows aren't blocked) on `shipments.freight_currency` (nullable), `shipments.invoice_currency` (NOT NULL), and `orders.order_currency` (NOT NULL), each requiring the value be one of `'TRY','EUR','USD','GBP'` — the same `BALANCE_CURRENCIES` enum the Zod layer enforces in `src/features/shipments/schema.ts` and `src/features/orders/schema.ts`. Also documenting the implicit convention used by the billing layer: **`shipments.invoice_currency` is the canonical currency for every monetary field on the shipment** — `shipment_billing`, `shipment_cogs`, and `shipment_freight` transactions are all written in this currency by `writeShipmentAccruals` in [src/features/shipments/billing.ts](src/features/shipments/billing.ts:194), and `order_details.unit_sales_price` / `est_purchase_unit_price` / `actual_purchase_price` are assumed to be in this same currency. Lines that need to be quoted in a different currency must be converted to the shipment's invoice currency before entry.
**Why:** A bug scan flagged that the DB accepted any text in these columns even though Zod restricts them, which would let a typo (e.g. `'UZZ'`) silently produce NaN downstream when FX-converted. Defensive constraints close that gap without requiring a backfill — `NOT VALID` enforces the rule for new writes immediately and historical rows can be validated separately once known clean. The COGS-currency convention isn't a code change; it was already the de-facto rule (the COGS amount is computed from numeric purchase prices that have no currency column and stored under `shipment.invoice_currency`), but it had never been written down. Future-me looking at `order_details` and wondering "what currency is `est_purchase_unit_price`?" should land on this entry.
**Alternatives:** (a) Add a `purchase_price_currency` column to `order_details` so each line carries its own currency — rejected for now; it expands the schema, would need a backfill, and the *real* operational rule is "you only put a line on a shipment if its prices are in the shipment's invoice currency", which is enforceable as a documented convention much more cheaply. Revisit if multi-currency lines on one shipment ever becomes a legitimate workflow. (b) Validate the existing rows immediately (`VALIDATE CONSTRAINT`) instead of `NOT VALID` — rejected; if any historical row violates the rule, the migration would fail loudly and block the deploy. `NOT VALID` lets the constraint take effect on writes immediately while leaving the historical-data check for a later, deliberate `VALIDATE` run. (c) Drop the constraint on `freight_currency` since the existing decision (2026-04-29 below) explicitly says freight_currency may legitimately differ from invoice_currency — kept; the constraint is about the *value* being a known currency code, not about it matching invoice_currency, so the two decisions don't conflict.

## 2026-05-05 — Audit log Phase 1: generic trigger on financial-impact tables
**What:** Added migration `20260505120000_audit_log.sql` introducing an append-only `public.audit_log` table (`id bigint identity`, `table_name`, `row_id text`, `action`, `old_data jsonb`, `new_data jsonb`, `changed_by uuid` from `auth.uid()`, `changed_at`) plus indexes on `(table_name, row_id, changed_at desc)` and `(changed_at desc)`. A single trigger function `public.log_audit()` (plpgsql, `security definer`, `search_path = pg_catalog, public`) is attached AFTER INSERT/UPDATE/DELETE on ten financial-impact tables: `transactions`, `treasury_movements`, `orders`, `order_details`, `shipments`, `loan_installments`, `psd_events`, `real_estate_installments`, `recurring_payment_occurrences` (all id-keyed), and `monthly_fx_overrides` (composite key passed as trigger args `('period','currency_code')`). The function reads PK columns from `TG_ARGV` (defaulting to `['id']`), serializes them as `value:value:…` for `row_id`, snapshots full row jsonb into `old_data`/`new_data`, and stamps `changed_by = auth.uid()`. Updates whose only differences are in `edited_by`/`edited_time`/`created_by`/`created_time` are skipped (deep-equal on the cleaned jsonb). The trigger soft-fails: an exception around the insert raises `WARNING` instead of aborting the source write. Immutability is enforced by `REVOKE UPDATE, DELETE ON public.audit_log FROM authenticated, anon` (RLS stays disabled to match the rest of the codebase). Phase 1 is capture-and-store only — no UI to read history yet.
**Why:** Until now, when something looks wrong on a financial table — a `transactions` row with the wrong amount, a `shipments` accrual that disagrees with the ledger, an `order_details` snapshot that drifted — there's no way to see who changed it or what it was before. The existing `created_by`/`edited_by`/`edited_time` columns are latest-touch only; prior values vanish on overwrite. **Trigger vs. app-level**: chose trigger so logging cannot be bypassed by a mutation that forgets to instrument itself, and so direct service-role SQL fixes (e.g. via the dashboard) are also captured — the whole point of the log is to catch the cases the app doesn't know about. **Generic vs. per-table**: chose one parameterized function — the composite-key table is handled via `TG_ARGV`, so the maintenance surface stays at one function regardless of how many tables are eventually instrumented. **Retention: keep forever.** Single-tenant, hundreds-not-millions of source rows; storage cost is negligible and a TTL job is one migration away if it ever becomes painful. **Soft-fail vs. hard-fail**: audit-table problems must not knock out finance writes. Audit completeness is a soft goal; a `RAISE WARNING` makes problems visible without blocking. **No-op skip is load-bearing.** `refreshShipmentAccruals` in [src/features/shipments/billing.ts](src/features/shipments/billing.ts) `update()`s `transactions` rows on every shipment refresh even when computed values are unchanged — without the deep-equal skip, every shipment edit would log 1–3 false-update rows. The skip ignores the four audit-stamp columns specifically because those *do* change on a no-op (the mutation layer always bumps `edited_time` and `edited_by`); the rest of the row is the source of truth for whether anything meaningful changed. **`auth.uid()` works in triggers** in this codebase because every app write goes through PostgREST as the `authenticated` role with a JWT — no `app.user_id` shim needed; in `AUTH_DISABLED` dev mode it correctly resolves to NULL, matching the existing `created_by` behavior. **Deferred tables (master data):** `contacts`, `products`, `partners`, `accounts`, `custody_locations`, `app_settings`, `expense_types`, `real_estate_deals`, `partner_loans`, `recurring_payments` (the *templates*, not the occurrences). These rarely change once seeded and the audit value is lower than for the rows that move money; deferring keeps Phase 1's blast radius tight. They can be added later by tacking on more `create trigger` statements in a follow-up migration. Verified end-to-end via a one-off script that exercised insert/update/no-op-update/delete on `psd_events`, composite-key insert/delete on `monthly_fx_overrides`, and a permission probe confirming the anon role gets a `42501 insufficient_privilege` on `delete from audit_log`. All 597 existing tests still pass.
**Alternatives:** (a) **App-level logging** in each mutation file — rejected; ten mutation files would each need to remember to call a logger before/after every write, the wrappers would have to deep-equal-diff in JS, and any direct SQL touch (dashboard fix, service-role script) would silently skip the log. The trigger captures the universe of writes by construction. (b) **One trigger function per table** with hand-written column lists — rejected; ten near-identical functions multiplies maintenance for no expressiveness gain, and `TG_ARGV` already covers the only schema variation we have (composite key). (c) **`uuid` PK on `audit_log`** — rejected in favor of `bigint identity`; an append-only ledger benefits from monotonic, smaller-index PKs and the rows are never referenced from elsewhere, so uuid's "globally unique without coordination" property buys nothing. (d) **`row_id uuid`** — rejected for the same reason composite keys exist on the list; `text` is uniformly correct and the `:` join is unambiguous because no PK column value contains `:` in any of the in-scope tables. (e) **Diff-only storage** (store just changed columns, not full rows) — rejected; full row snapshots are larger but trivially decodable into "what did it look like at time T", which is the actual use case for Phase 2 (per-row history view). Diffs would need a reconstruction step at read time. (f) **`SECURITY INVOKER` instead of `SECURITY DEFINER`** — rejected; the audit insert needs to succeed regardless of whether the writing role has insert privileges on `audit_log`. Definer + soft-fail + `set search_path` is the standard hardening pattern. (g) **Hard-fail on log insert** — rejected per the soft-goal rationale above; if the audit table goes wrong, the *finance* layer must keep functioning. The `RAISE WARNING` ensures we still find out. (h) **Including `recurring_payments` (templates) in Phase 1** — rejected; templates are master-data-shaped (rarely changed, set up once) and the materialized `recurring_payment_occurrences` is where the real money-touching writes happen. (i) **`for each statement` triggers** to log batched updates — rejected; we want one row per affected source row, which is exactly what `for each row` gives us. Statement-level would lose the per-row jsonb snapshots that make the log useful for reconstruction.

## 2026-04-24 — Rolled-over lines always appear on shipment statements at €0, labeled with original billing shipment

## 2026-04-24 — Payment log on the statement shows only FIFO-allocated-to-this-shipment portions
**What:** The "Paiements reçus" table on each statement is filtered to the subset of FIFO `payment_allocations` whose `related_shipment_id` equals the current shipment. **Why:** A statement is per-shipment; showing the customer's full payment history on every shipment's relevé would be confusing and would leak cross-shipment balances onto single-shipment documents. The FIFO engine already decides which dollars pay which invoice — reusing that allocation for the document keeps the statement's math identical to what the internal ledger shows, so there's one source of truth the client and we can both reconcile against.
**Alternatives:** Show every customer payment in full on every statement — rejected, same payment would appear on N shipments with confusing totals. ## 2026-04-24 — Statement PDFs stored with timestamp suffix, no deletion of prior versions
**What:** Each generation writes to `shipment-invoices/{shipment_id}/statement-{unix_timestamp}.pdf` with `upsert: false` — a unique path every time. "Regenerate" is therefore always non-destructive.
**Why:** Statements are legal-adjacent documents the customer may have already received and acted on; overwriting the blob means we can no longer prove what we sent when they email back asking about line 4. Storage is cheap — a handful of PDFs per shipment, measured in hundreds of KB each, is free at our scale. A timestamp-suffixed path is the simplest possible audit trail: one directory listing tells the full regeneration history, no separate DB table needed. Point-in-time reconstruction is free.
**Alternatives:** `upsert: true` at a deterministic path (like the proforma does) — rejected, the proforma is a pre-contract document that only matters in its latest form; the statement is a post-contract record we need to be able to re-send as it was. Store all versions in a `statement_pdfs` child table with metadata — rejected, over-engineered when the storage path already encodes the timestamp. Soft-delete old files after N days — rejected, introduces a cleanup rule that could retroactively destroy the audit trail we built the column for.

**Alternatives:** Separate `order_proformas` table keyed by `order_id` — rejected, over-engineered for a 1:1 relationship that never needs history. JSONB `proforma_metadata` column — rejected, loses per-field constraints, breaks the status-gate SQL, and Yusuf wants the fields to show up in the auto-generated TypeScript types.

## 2026-04-23 — Offer numbers are auto-generated as `TG-YYYYMMDD-NNN`, no manual override
**What:** When saving proforma metadata for the first time, the mutation computes `offer_number = 'TG-' + YYYYMMDD(offer_date in Europe/Istanbul) + '-' + zero-padded sequence`. Sequence is derived from `SELECT count(*) FROM orders WHERE offer_number LIKE 'TG-{YYYYMMDD}-%' + 1`. Write is atomic with the rest of the metadata UPDATE; on Postgres unique-violation (`23505`) the mutation retries once, which is cheaper than distributed locking and good enough for single-user MVP volumes.
**Why:** A human-readable date-prefixed counter is what Yusuf and his customers already expect on paper offers. Generating it server-side at the moment of save guarantees it's set exactly when proforma details become meaningful — never before (inquiry orders have no offer) and never after (once quoted, the number is locked). Istanbul TZ anchors the date to the business's working day rather than UTC. No manual override because permitting user-typed offer numbers invites collisions and formatting drift; if that ever becomes a real ask, a DB sequence is the clean migration path.
**Alternatives:** Postgres SEQUENCE — rejected, can't easily re-anchor per day. UUID — rejected, unreadable on printed proformas. Client-side counter with user confirmation — rejected, races across tabs/users are worse than the single retry we do now.

## 2026-04-23 — `inquiry → quoted` transition is gated on proforma metadata being filled

Gating at the transition rather than at the Generate-proforma click forces the data to exist before downstream code paths (PDF generation, customer-facing communication) assume it. Sharing the rule between gate and button tooltip prevents drift: one source of truth for "is this order proforma-ready?" Keeping the error message explicit about which fields are missing means the user doesn't have to guess.
**Alternatives:** Gate only at the Generate button — rejected, lets the order sit in `quoted` without meaningful metadata. Gate on every proforma field — rejected, the optional `proforma_notes_*` rows are genuinely optional and shouldn't block status progression. Infer missing fields in the toast at call time — rejected, duplicates the logic the button already needs.

## 2026-04-23 — Proforma PDF uses `@react-pdf/renderer`, rendered client-side, stored at a deterministic path
**What:** PDF generation runs in the browser via `pdf(<ProformaDocument data={...} />).toBlob()` with a dynamic `await import('@react-pdf/renderer')` so the ~400KB library stays out of the order-detail route's initial chunk. The document tree is a tiny React component library under `src/lib/pdf/` (header, client/offer block, line table, notes block). Deterministic path (`{order_id}/proforma/{offer_number}.pdf`) means regenerations overwrite predictably, no orphan-file sprawl, and the storage bucket stays browsable by eye. Using the existing `order-attachments` bucket means one less storage policy to manage.

## 2026-04-23 — FIFO allocation engine is pure, client-side, runs per view
**What:** A pure function `allocateFifo(events, displayCurrency)` lives at `src/lib/ledger/fifo-allocation.ts`. It takes a customer's full transaction history (billings, payments, refunds, adjustments) and returns per-shipment paid/outstanding amounts plus unallocated credit. No DB calls, no caching — runs fresh on every ledger view. Events whose currency can't be normalized to `displayCurrency` (no frozen FX available) are skipped with an attached reason, not silently dropped.
**Why:** A pure function is trivially testable, trivially composable (same engine powers both the shipment card and the ledger page), and cheap to run on the tens-to-hundreds of rows a customer's ledger typically holds. Centralizing the allocation rule in one place means every view agrees on what "paid" means for a given shipment — no drift between the shipment card and the ledger.
**Alternatives:** SQL-side allocation via window functions — rejected, harder to test and reason about, and the customer volumes don't need SQL-side optimization. Store per-shipment paid amounts as a materialized column — rejected, would drift every time a payment was added or edited and require triggers to keep in sync.

## 2026-04-23 — Rolled-in credit is not a separate ledger row; it's emergent from FIFO
**What:** When a shipment's invoice shows a credit carried over from a prior overpayment (e.g. "−€446.57 from Shipment 7"), that credit is not stored as a row anywhere. It's the natural output of running `allocateFifo` over the customer's full ledger: the prior shipment's excess payment spills forward into the next shipment's `paid_amount`, automatically reducing its outstanding.
**Why:** A stored "rolled-in credit" row would duplicate information already derivable from the ledger and would drift if payments or billings were later edited. Emergent-via-FIFO keeps one source of truth and one calculation path.
**Alternatives:** Write a synthetic `adjustment` row at each rollover — rejected, fictional events pollute the ledger and are hard to explain to an accountant. Store a per-shipment rolled_in_credit column — rejected, drifts.

## 2026-04-23 — Events in non-normalizable currencies are skipped with visible warnings, never auto-converted at display time
**What:** If a transaction's currency can't be expressed in the display currency (no `fx_converted_amount` / `fx_target_currency` match), the FIFO engine attaches it to a `skipped_events` array. The ledger and shipment-card views render these events greyed-out with a warning icon and tooltip, but they do not contribute to any total. Live FX rates are not applied to historical events.
**Why:** Applying live FX to past events would violate the frozen-FX principle established in the transactions module (2026-04-23). Silently dropping such events would hide them from the user. Visible warnings force the user to either add frozen FX to the transaction (via edit) or accept that the event is excluded from the balance math.
**Alternatives:** Auto-convert using the nearest fx_snapshot — rejected, re-introduces drift. Silently exclude — rejected, data loss invisible to the user.

## 2026-04-23 — Shipment billing transaction is written at draft → booked, then refreshed in place on every line/freight/order-assignment change
**What:** Booking a shipment inserts a single `transactions` row with `kind='shipment_billing'`, `amount = SUM(order_details.quantity * unit_sales_price for orders where billing_shipment_id = this shipment AND status != 'cancelled') + COALESCE(shipment.freight_cost, 0)`, currency from `shipment.invoice_currency`, and `related_shipment_id` populated. Every subsequent edit that could affect that total (add/update/delete order line, cancel order, assign-to-shipment, freight cost change, batch import) re-computes the total and UPDATEs the same row in place — never inserts a second row. Audit stamps (`edited_by`, `edited_time`) advance on every refresh. Once a shipment is `arrived`, all billing-affecting edits throw "Cannot modify billing amount on arrived shipment. Use an adjustment transaction instead."
**Why:** The customer ledger needs one row per shipment, not a stream of deltas — debits and credits in this business are coarse-grained (one invoice per shipment), and a single source-of-truth row keeps the UI summary, the customer balance calculation, and any future PDF invoice rendering trivial. Refreshing in place avoids a "billing history" sub-ledger that nobody asked for. Locking at `arrived` mirrors how a paper invoice works once it leaves the office: corrections happen as separate adjustment transactions, not by editing the original.
**Alternatives:** Append a new transaction per change (delta ledger) — rejected, fragments customer balance reads and complicates "what's the current invoice for this shipment" lookups. Defer billing write until `in_transit` (matches when goods leave) — rejected, customers expect the proforma invoice immediately on booking, not when the container ships. Compute total live without a stored row — rejected, breaks the customer ledger view which expects rows, and loses the immutable record needed once the shipment arrives.

## 2026-04-23 — Order status cascade to `shipped` happens at booked → in_transit, hard-blocked by inquiry/quoted orders
**What:** Advancing a shipment from `booked` to `in_transit` updates every linked order whose status is `accepted` or `in_production` to `shipped`, in a single app-level pseudo-transaction (per-order updates with rollback on any failure, plus rollback of the billing-amount refresh). If any linked order is still in `inquiry` or `quoted`, the transition throws with the offending order IDs in the message — the user must resolve those orders first. Orders already in `shipped`, `delivered`, or `cancelled` are left untouched. Per-order delivery to the end customer happens manually later (no auto-cascade at `arrived`).
**Why:** A shipment in transit means every order on it has, by definition, been shipped — keeping order status in sync with shipment status without manual per-order clicks reflects reality. Hard-blocking on inquiry/quoted (rather than silently skipping or auto-accepting) protects against the user accidentally putting unfinished negotiations on a real shipment: those orders shouldn't be on the manifest at all, and the loud failure is the right signal. The atomic-rollback pattern matches the proforma-import precedent — Supabase's JS client doesn't expose multi-statement transactions, so we approximate atomicity in app code.
**Alternatives:** Silently skip non-cascadable orders — rejected, hides a data-quality problem the user needs to see. Auto-promote inquiry/quoted to accepted as part of the cascade — rejected, that decision belongs to the sales side, not the logistics side. Cascade at `arrived` instead of `in_transit` — rejected, "shipped" semantically means "left the origin", not "reached the destination".

## 2026-04-23 — in_transit → arrived is operational metadata only; no order or billing side effects
**What:** Advancing a shipment from `in_transit` to `arrived` only flips the shipment's status. Linked orders stay at `shipped` — they are not auto-promoted to `delivered`. The billing transaction is not refreshed (and any further edit that would refresh it is blocked by the `arrived` lock). Per-order delivery confirmation, partial delivery, and any post-arrival adjustments are out of scope for this wave; they will be handled by future per-order UI plus adjustment transactions.
**Why:** Arrival at port ≠ delivery to customer. A container can sit at customs for weeks, deliveries can be partial, and individual orders may be released to the customer on different dates. Auto-flipping every order to `delivered` on arrival would invent data the system doesn't actually know. Locking billing at this point matches the principle that the original invoice is now a historical document — corrections live in adjustment transactions, not in edits to the original row.
**Alternatives:** Auto-cascade orders `shipped → delivered` on arrival — rejected, fabricates delivery confirmations the business doesn't have. Allow continued billing edits past `arrived` — rejected, undermines the "invoice is final once goods land" principle and would require versioning the billing row. Treat `arrived` as a no-op status (skip it entirely) — rejected, the operational milestone is genuinely useful for tracking and is the natural anchor for the billing lock.

## 2026-04-23 — Proforma import: user-driven external LLM + JSON paste, no app-side LLM calls

The manual copy-paste step happens only 4–5 times per month, so friction cost is low. The prompt template is rendered live from the Zod schema, so schema changes automatically propagate to the prompt the user sees.
**Alternatives:** Server-side extraction via the Anthropic API — rejected for cost and key management. Client-side extraction — rejected, exposes the key. Manual single-line entry — rejected, doesn't scale to 40-line proformas.

## 2026-04-23 — Every proforma line creates a new product; no matching against existing catalog

The user explicitly opted for this simplicity; duplicates, if they appear, will be cleaned up manually later.

## 2026-04-23 — Proforma PDF not stored; ingestion is ephemeral
**What:** The proforma PDF itself is never uploaded to the app. Only the extracted JSON is pasted in, and even that is discarded after the atomic insert completes. No `supplier_proformas` table, no storage bucket, no attached file on any order.
**Why:** The user explicitly chose Option 1 ("pure batch-import UI, no stored entity") in Session 1. The value is in the resulting order lines, not in the source document. If source-document tracking ever becomes useful later, promotion to a stored entity is non-breaking.

## 2026-04-23 — Proforma import mutation: insert-then-rollback-on-failure, not a single PG transaction
**What:** The batch mutation creates products and order lines via sequential Supabase inserts, tracking created ids. On any failure, it deletes everything it already created. This is not a true ACID transaction, but approximates atomicity at the application level.
**Why:** Supabase's JS client doesn't expose multi-statement transactions. The alternatives (a PL/pgSQL function the user runs once, or a server-side transaction via a custom endpoint) add operational complexity disproportionate to the risk. Inserts of newly-minted rows are the safe case for this pattern: there's nothing for the rollback to race against.

## 2026-04-23 — V1 `orders`, `order_details`, `shipments` dropped and rebuilt from scratch

Keep V1 alongside V2 — rejected, two tables answering the same question. Patch V1 incrementally — rejected, would force the new constraints (snapshot columns, CHECK invariants, new FKs) to live as optional, defeating the point.

## 2026-04-23 — Inquiry is an order status, not a separate entity
**What:** Orders use a single 7-value `status` enum (`inquiry`, `quoted`, `accepted`, `in_production`, `shipped`, `delivered`, `cancelled`). A brand-new customer inquiry is created as an `orders` row with `status='inquiry'` and zero or more `order_details` lines; it progresses through quoted/accepted on the same row. No separate `inquiries` or `quotes` table.
**Why:** Every inquiry either becomes a real order or gets cancelled — splitting into a separate table would force a "promote inquiry to order" copy step that loses line-item continuity and doubles the surface area for product snapshots, attachments, and status history. Treating it as a status keeps the lifecycle linear and keeps all documents/attachments on one row.
**Alternatives:** Separate `inquiries` table that morphs into an `orders` row — rejected, introduces a schema-level transition and splits queries. Treat quoted-but-not-accepted as a draft flag on orders — rejected, flags don't compose into a status pipeline; three-plus flags would be needed.

## 2026-04-23 — Order lines snapshot the product at line-creation time; later product edits don't propagate
**What:** `order_details` carries `product_name_snapshot`, `product_description_snapshot`, `product_photo_snapshot`, `unit_snapshot`, `cbm_per_unit_snapshot`, `weight_kg_per_unit_snapshot`, and the 5 packaging fields (`packaging_type`, `package_{length,width,height}_cm`, `units_per_package`). These are populated from the `products` row at the moment the line is added and are independently editable per line. The FK `product_id` stays on the row for joins, but reports of "what was on that order" read the snapshot columns.
**Why:** A quoted/accepted order is a contract with the customer. If the operator renames a product, adjusts packaging, or swaps photos on the Products page, historical quotes must still reproduce exactly what was offered. Snapshotting makes the order row self-describing; it also lets operators intentionally override a field per-line (e.g. custom box dims for one shipment) without creating a product variant.
**Alternatives:** Read-through joins to `products` at display time — rejected, a later product edit silently rewrites the history of every order. Versioned products table — rejected, heavy schema for a small set of editable fields; snapshotting is the minimum that solves the problem.

## 2026-04-23 — Orders carry two shipment FKs: `shipment_id` (physical) and `billing_shipment_id` (financial)
**What:** `orders.shipment_id` points at the shipment the goods physically travel on; `orders.billing_shipment_id` points at the shipment whose invoice line the order is billed under. On initial assign, `billing_shipment_id` defaults to match `shipment_id`. When a shipment is booked but an order rolls over to a later shipment, the physical pointer moves but the billing pointer stays, so the customer is billed on the original shipment.
**Why:** "Ship it next month but bill it on this month's proforma" is a real operator workflow — the customer has already committed to paying on the earlier shipment's invoice even though production slipped. Collapsing into one FK would force a choice: either the invoice rewrites when orders roll over (wrong), or the shipping manifest lies about what's on the boat (also wrong). Two FKs is the minimum that models both facts independently.
**Alternatives:** Single `shipment_id` with a boolean "billed-separately" flag — rejected, can't point at a different shipment. Per-shipment `invoice_lines` with explicit order→line mapping — rejected, heavier schema and doesn't solve the common case any better than two FKs.

## 2026-04-23 — Cancelling an order detaches from both shipments (NULLs `shipment_id` and `billing_shipment_id`)
**What:** The `cancelOrder` mutation sets `status='cancelled'`, stamps `cancelled_at=now()` and `cancellation_reason`, and clears both `shipment_id` and `billing_shipment_id` in the same update. A DB CHECK `(status='cancelled') = (cancelled_at IS NOT NULL)` guarantees status/timestamp stay consistent even if a future code path bypasses the mutation.
**Why:** A cancelled order shouldn't show up in shipment manifests or invoice totals — leaving the FKs set would require every shipment-level aggregate to filter `status != 'cancelled'`, and the "orders in this shipment" list would need to hide cancelled rows everywhere. Nulling on cancel makes cancelled orders invisible to shipment queries by default; the `cancellation_reason` + `cancelled_at` columns preserve the audit trail.
**Alternatives:** Leave FKs set and filter on status everywhere — rejected, every join has to remember the filter and one missed spot leaks cancelled orders into totals. Block cancellation once assigned — rejected, real cancellations happen after assignment all the time.

## 2026-04-23 — Shipment `draft → booked` gated on ≥1 linked order; later transitions ungated (Wave 1)
**What:** `advanceShipmentStatus` refuses `draft → booked` unless at least one order has `shipment_id = shipment.id`. `booked → in_transit` and `in_transit → arrived` currently advance the status only — the Wave 3 side effects (write a `shipment_billing` transaction, cascade linked order statuses to `shipped`/`delivered`) are stubbed with inline `TODO(Wave 3)` comments at the transition sites.
**Why:** Booking an empty shipment is always a mistake — the operator forgot to attach the orders first — so the gate catches it at the cheapest point. The later transitions deliberately ship without cascades in Wave 1 because the invoice/billing rules are still being finalized; stubbing keeps the UI operable while preventing accidental half-built billing logic from going live.
**Alternatives:** Allow empty bookings — rejected, always a user error. Wire all cascades now — rejected, shipment_billing transaction format and currency rules aren't locked yet; half-correct cascades would corrupt data that's hard to unwind.

## 2026-04-23 — Two separate storage buckets: `order-attachments` and `shipment-documents`
**What:** Orders use a private bucket `order-attachments` with path `{order_id}/{type}/{filename}` where `type ∈ {customer_po, proposal}`. Shipments use a private bucket `shipment-documents` with path `{shipment_id}/{timestamp}.{ext}` (single `documents_file` column, replace-not-append). Both use the deferred-upload + signed-URL pattern already proven on transactions and products.
**Why:** Retention and access patterns differ. ## 2026-04-23 — FX captured on client_payment at transaction time and frozen

## 2026-04-21 — Unified accounts: one table for every asset type
**What:** The `accounts` table holds fiat bank balances, crypto, physical metals, and fund positions — rows tagged by `asset_type` (fiat | crypto | metal | fund). Old `currency` column renamed `asset_code` since it now holds BTC, Altın, KTJ, etc., not just currencies.
**Why:** Treasury.md's "quantity is the source of truth" principle applies equally to grams of gold and units of KTJ as it does to dollars. Splitting into per-asset-class tables would duplicate schema for the same pattern.
**Alternatives:** Separate `bank_accounts`, `crypto_holdings`, `fund_positions` tables — rejected, same shape repeated three times.

## 2026-04-21 — Table name stays `accounts`, not renamed to `holdings`
**What:** Keep `accounts` as the table name even though treasury.md's sketch calls it "Holdings."
**Why:** Many V1 references (transactions.from_account_id, to_account_id) already point at `accounts`. Renaming would cascade through the codebase for cosmetic gain.

## 2026-04-21 — Custody location as editable lookup (Şirket / Ortak / Kasa + off-site)
**What:** New `custody_locations` table (id, name, location_type: bank | physical). Accounts link to it via `custody_location_id`. List is editable in the app — Yusuf can add GAP 1/4, Kasa Çekme, etc. without a migration.
**Why:** Treasury.md requires custody to be a first-class dimension independent of asset type. Editability avoids schema changes every time a new safe or off-site location is added.

## 2026-04-21 — Price snapshots unified across asset types
**What:** Single `price_snapshots` table (asset_code, snapshot_date, price, price_currency, source). V1's `fund_prices` migrated in and dropped.
**Why:** Gold, crypto, and fund prices all have the same shape — a daily price in some currency with a source. One table keeps the display logic uniform.

## 2026-04-21 — FX snapshots separate from price snapshots
**What:** `fx_snapshots(currency_code, snapshot_date, rate_to_usd, source, fetched_at)`. Written only when the user taps the FX refresh button (per treasury.md — no background refresh).
**Why:** FX has two distinct jobs (wallet display vs. frozen client-balance rate) and a different refresh cadence than asset prices. Combining them would conflate "price of an asset" with "value of a currency against USD."

## 2026-04-21 — Ortak movement type deferred to accounting module
**What:** The partner-loan vs. profit-share distinction on Ortak transactions is not modelled yet. Will be added to the `transactions` table when the accounting module is designed.
**Why:** Mainstream ERPs handle this via GL account codes, not transaction flags. That design belongs to accounting, not treasury.

## 2026-04-21 — Contact types: customer, supplier, logistics, other
**What:** Contact `type` constrained to these four values. Government dropped. Partners live in their own table, not in contacts.
**Why:** Mainstream ERPs don't treat governments as contacts — tax offices are registries referenced in filings, not transactable parties. Partners have their own data shape (ownership %, profit-share) and their own UI section.
**Alternatives:** Keep Government (rejected — no transactions attach to it). Fold Partners into contacts (rejected — different fields, already separate in the app).

## 2026-04-21 — Single type per contact
**What:** One contact has exactly one type. A company that is both supplier and customer is duplicated.
**Why:** Has never happened in Yusuf's practice. Multi-role modelling would cost UI and schema complexity for a non-problem.
**Alternatives:** Multi-role via array or join table — rejected.

## 2026-04-21 — Country as controlled ISO list
**What:** `contacts.country_code` references a `countries` lookup table keyed by ISO-3166-1 alpha-2 codes (TR, ML, CM…). Lookup holds display name and flag.
**Why:** Free text breaks country-grouped views and flag rendering (Türkiye / Turkey / TR become three groups).
**Alternatives:** Free text (rejected).

## 2026-04-21 — Tax ID stored on all contacts; Tax Office Turkish-only in UI
**What:** `tax_id` is a field on every contact, required for customers (needed in Yükleme Talimatı), optional otherwise. `tax_office` is stored on every contact but only shown in forms when country = Türkiye.
**Why:** Yükleme Talimatı requires client tax ID regardless of country. Vergi Dairesi (tax office) is a Turkish tax concept only — showing it for foreign contacts clutters the form.
**Alternatives:** Tax ID only for Turkish contacts (rejected — foreign customers need it too for shipping docs).

## 2026-04-21 — Contact notes split: static field + timestamped activity log
**What:** Each contact has a `notes` text field for persistent facts, plus a separate `contact_notes` table for timestamped interaction entries. Activity entries are append-only.
**Why:** Static context ("pays late") and dated incidents ("21 Apr disagreement") are different needs. Mixing them loses the date anchor on incidents.
**Alternatives:** Single notes field with hand-typed dates (rejected — unstructured, not queryable).

## 2026-04-21 — Packaging defaults on the product, overridable per order
**What:** Each product has a usual packaging config. When added to an order line, that config copies over and can be edited for that specific order without touching the product master.
**Why:** Most products always ship the same way (tiles on 120×80 pallets, etc.), so a default covers nearly all cases. Occasional one-offs (break a pallet into loose boxes) need to be captured without polluting the product master.
**Alternatives:** (a) packaging only on product — too rigid; (b) only on order line — repetitive entry; (c) only on shipment — too late, proposal needs it.

## 2026-04-21 — Product versioning: snapshot at order time (Option A)
**What:** `products` table holds only the current state. When a product is added to an order line, relevant fields (name, price, description, photo, packaging) are copied onto the line. Editing a product later doesn't retroactively change old orders.
**Why:** Old orders must stay faithful to what was sold. Snapshotting keeps schema simple (one row per product) while preserving historical truth.
**Alternatives:** Option B — version rows per material edit, order lines reference a specific version. Heavier schema, allows a product-history view. Rejected because per-order fidelity matters, product-side history doesn't.

## 2026-04-21 — Inquiry is the first status of an order, not a separate entity
**What:** An order can exist from the inquiry stage with minimal fields (client + product). It progresses: inquiry → quoted → accepted → in production → shipped → done.
**Why:** Avoids two near-identical entities and a "promote inquiry to order" step. Same row, earlier state.

## 2026-04-21 — Client balance tracked per client, not per order
**What:** One running balance per client. Payments and order charges flow against it. No per-order balance.
**Why:** Clients pre-load funds to build up for future orders ($30k sent when only $10k of orders exist). Per-order balance would be over-engineered for this pattern.

## 2026-04-21 — Client balance currency fixed per client
**What:** Each client has a default currency. Their balance is always kept in that currency. Payments in other currencies convert at receipt-time rate.
**Why:** One consistent view of what the client has paid and what's owed. Avoids multi-currency-per-client mess.

## 2026-04-21 — Payment FX rate captured at transaction time
**What:** When a payment arrives, the FX rate used to convert it into the client's balance currency is stored on the transaction row and never recomputed.
**Why:** A client-balance credit is a promise made on a specific date. It must not silently move when rates change. Distinct from treasury wallet display, where FX is applied at display time from snapshots.

## 2026-04-21 — Freight is a separate line, not allocated into per-product profit
**What:** Orders are sold EXW. Freight is quoted and invoiced as its own line, not amortised back into product margins.
**Why:** Matches how Yusuf already sells. Keeps per-product profit clean.

## 2026-04-21 — Orders sit inside shipments; shipping the shipment ships all orders inside
**What:** A shipment groups one or more orders. Order status is per order, but when the shipment ships, every order inside transitions together.
**Why:** One container often carries multiple consolidated orders. Status must cascade from the shipment event.
